I was born and raised in Jhapa, a small town in eastern Nepal. From a young age, I was a naturally curious person, always eager to understand the world around me. Even then, I dreamed of becoming a scientist, though that path wasn’t without its challenges. Growing up in a place with limited access to English-medium education, I often had to work twice as hard to bridge the gap. I devoured any book I could find, each page expanding my understanding of the world. Eventually, it became clear that to pursue the kind of education I wanted, I would have to leave my hometown.

Driven by a thirst for knowledge, I was fortunate enough to study and travel internationally, each new experience introducing me to ideas that reshaped my worldview. During this journey, I was drawn to atheistic philosophies and scientific reasoning, reading books like The Selfish Gene by Richard Dawkins and other philosophical works that questioned the nature of existence. It was during this phase that I became captivated by the realm of pure research, finding purpose in exploring fundamental scientific questions and probing the mysteries of the universe.

During this journey, I also had the privilege of co-authoring a book titled The Realist. The book focused on promoting critical thinking and encouraging skepticism, aiming to inspire young students to approach the world through a scientific lens.

To further advance scientific thinking and popularize science in Nepal, I started a nonprofit organization called Reason and Science Projects. Through this organization, we organized workshops and seminars emphasizing the importance of scientific thinking, engaging students and communities across the country. This initiative not only sparked greater interest in science but also helped create a foundational understanding of its importance in daily life. Beyond that, we partnered with the International Center for Theoretical Physics (ICTP) to secure funding to construct a school in a remote part of Nepal. The school was damaged during the Earthquake that hit Nepal in 2015.

During my Master’s studies, I undertook a challenging project focused on the precession of Mercury’s orbit. My goal was to understand how surface tension corrections could influence this celestial motion, a line of inquiry that ultimately resulted in a research paper. While I had always been fascinated by astrophysics and considered mathematics one of my strengths, my early interests hadn’t directly led me toward condensed matter physics. My journey there began with exploratory questions, like investigating perturbative corrections in a helium atom due to nonlinear defects. Through experiences like this, I began to see the beauty and depth of condensed matter physics, which, over time, grew from a distant interest into a true passion.

Today, my research explores the interactions between particles and fields, uncovering the complex and sometimes unpredictable ways they behave. Non-linear dynamics, in particular, has become central to my work as I seek to understand how even simple systems can evolve into profoundly intricate patterns.

My work is not merely about answering questions; it’s about contributing to something larger—a quest to unveil the laws of nature that govern everything around us.
